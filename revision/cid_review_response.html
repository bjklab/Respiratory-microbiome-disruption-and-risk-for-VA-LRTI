R1 This work has been presented as a poster that has been published in "Infection Control & Hospital Epidemiology" in November 2020. This should be detailed in the manuscript. In addition, one of the results from the abstract (from the poster) seems to differ significantly from the present data and conclusion: "In this cohort, VAE risk was paradoxically associated with higher admission Shannon diversity and lower admission maximum ASV abundance."... Could the author comments? To clarify: we presented a different population and a different outcome in the poster cited by the reviewer. Our poster analyzed how a single measure of the respiratory microbiome at the time of hospital admission (i.e., 83 admission specimens) could predict adverse ventilator-associated events (VAE), a surveillance definition that has been associated with a range of adverse outcomes of mechanical ventilation. The manuscript under review is a comprehensive, longitudinal analysis (1066 specimens) of the respiratory microbiome over time. In this manuscript, we are focused on dynamic respiratory bacterial community disruption over extended periods of mechanical ventilation and on the association between dynamic microbiome disruption and infection (i.e., VA-LRTI). To summarize: the disparity between the results quoted by the reviewer and the results presented in this manuscript is attributable to the fact that both the population and outcome under study are different. We have clarified this point and cited the prior poster presentation in the manuscript. Materials and Methods, fourth paragraph.

R2 There may be an important interaction between bacterial abundance (measured via qPCR) and diversity indices. Generally tracheal aspirates are relatively high-biomass (by respiratory microbiome standards), but it's possible that some were at or below the limit of detection for 16S sequencing (in which case diversity indices may be confounded by "background" taxa from reagent contamination). I know the analytic focus here was on predictors of LRTI, but given the opportunity here, I wonder if the authors could explore whether these two dimensions might inform the interpretation of each other. Examples might be: 1) asking if community composition (via 16S) varies with bacterial abundance (via qPCR), perhaps with likely pathogens enriched in high-abundance specimens and likely contaminants enriched in low-abundance specimens; or 2) asking if the predictive power of diversity is greater if analysis is restricted to specimenwith qPCR values above a threshold; or 3) asking if (e.g.) the product of bacterial abundance and community domination is more predictive than either individual index. These are merely suggestions for possible exploratory analyses; I have no objection to the straightforward way the authors interrogated these indices in isolation. We thank the reviewer for raising this important point. We have added extensive new analyses to examine the potential interaction between total bacterial abundance (16S qPCR) and other measures of bacterial community composition, as well as comparisons of univariable and multivariable models perform in the prediction of VA-LRTI. Results, (new) sixth paragraph. Discussion, first paragraph.
R2 As mentioned above, the authors do an adequate job of dealing with the "no gold standard" problem that is inherent to studying LRTIs ("Definition of Outcome" in Methods). The authors revisit this again late in the mansuscript as a limitation, but I'd invite more discussion, as this strikes me as a critical problem for the field and potentially a very low ceiling when it comes to showing the utility of these novel indices of developing infection. If we are assessing our techniques and models by their ability to predict a very imprecise and subjective outcome (e.g. contingent on provider decision to send respiratory cultures), then it seems improbable that we'll ever be able to "prove" diagnostic or prognostic utility to them. E.g. if even inter-observer variation is poor for a diagnosis (as it is for the assessment of LRTA), how will a novel approach ever perform well? It seems plausible that high-dimensional molecular measurements such as those used by the authors will soon be more informative than subjective clinical judgment; how will we translate them into clinical use given this barrier? I have no solution, but would invite the authors to expand on this discussion given that it has rather pressing importance for the implications of this study. We thank the reviewer for this suggestion. We have revised the Discussion to better discuss the potential for improvement in this area. Discussion, last paragraph.
